Common Mistakes When Baking or Cooking at 180 Centigrade in an Oven

Using 180 Centigrade without adjusting for fan or convection oven

Result: Many home bakers set their oven to 180 Centigrade for recipes designed for conventional ovens, forgetting that fan ovens circulate hot air more efficiently, which can lead to food burning on the outside while the inside cooks unevenly.
Solution: Always reduce the oven temperature by approximately 20 Centigrade when using a fan oven, and monitor the cooking progress carefully with a thermometer to ensure proper heat penetration throughout your food.

Confusing Fahrenheit and Centigrade when setting the oven

Result: A common error is assuming 180 Centigrade is the same as 180 Fahrenheit. This mistake causes the oven to be far too cool or excessively hot, resulting in baked goods with burnt edges, undercooked centers, or uneven texture.
Solution: Check the recipe carefully for the unit used, convert accurately using a Centigrade to Fahrenheit conversion chart if necessary, and always confirm with an oven thermometer.

Not preheating the oven to the full 180 Centigrade

Result: Placing food in an oven that has not fully reached 180 Centigrade leads to uneven cooking and can affect rising in cakes or browning in roasted vegetables.
Solution: Preheat the oven fully to 180 Centigrade before putting in your ingredients and wait for the indicator or thermometer to confirm the set temperature is reached.

Using incorrect rack positions when baking at 180 Centigrade

Result: Placing baking trays too close to the top heat source can burn the tops of cakes or cookies, while placing them on the bottom rack can result in undercooked tops and uneven browning.
Solution: Use the middle rack for most baking at 180 Centigrade, and rotate trays halfway through cooking to ensure even heat exposure.

Ignoring the impact of pan type and color at 180 Centigrade

Result: Dark or metal pans absorb more heat, cooking food faster, whereas glass or light-colored pans cook slower. Using 180 Centigrade indiscriminately can lead to burnt edges or undercooked interiors.
Solution: Adjust baking times or slightly lower the temperature for darker pans and monitor cooking closely.

Overcrowding the oven when baking at 180 Centigrade

Result: Placing too many trays or items at once reduces airflow and heat circulation, resulting in uneven cooking and inconsistent browning across the oven.
Solution: Leave space between trays and bake in batches if necessary to maintain proper temperature and airflow at 180 Centigrade.

Not considering food thickness or density when baking at 180 Centigrade

Result: Thicker cakes, loaves, or roasts require longer baking times at 180 Centigrade, while thin cookies or pastries finish quickly. Ignoring thickness causes undercooked centers or overbrowned exteriors.
Solution: Adjust baking time according to the food's size and thickness, checking internal temperature where possible for optimal results.
